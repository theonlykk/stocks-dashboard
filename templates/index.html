What is this dashboard?

Two equities are cointegrated when their log prices tend to move together over time — not perfectly in lockstep, but anchored by a long-run relationship. When one stock drifts too far from that relationship, it tends to snap back. This dashboard identifies those moments and shows the historical track record of trading that reversion.

The heatmap shows which pairs within each GICS sector are currently cointegrated — green means the relationship is statistically significant. Click any cell to load the full analysis for that pair: log-price history, spread dynamics, trade signals, a simulated backtest, and a live trade recommendation if conditions are met right now.

All prices are log-transformed before regression. The spread is log(P₁) − β · log(P₂) — a dimensionless log-return residual. PnL is expressed in log-spread units. Pairs are sourced from GICS sectors via yfinance.

Parameters
Sector
i
Filter to a single GICS sector. Pairs within the same sector share common risk factors, making cointegration more economically meaningful.
Window
i
Number of trading days used for each rolling calculation. 126 days (6 months) balances stability and responsiveness.
P-Value Threshold
i
Only pairs below this threshold are highlighted as cointegrated. 0.05 means a 5% chance the relationship is random. Lower = more selective.
Entry Trigger (σ)
i
How far the spread must deviate (in σ) before a trade is entered. A second position is added automatically at entry + 1σ.
Profit Target (σ per leg)
i
Each leg exits when z-score reverts by this amount from its entry. e.g. entered at -1σ with 0.5σ target exits at -0.5σ.
Hurst Max
i
Maximum Hurst exponent allowed for trade entry. Values below 0.5 indicate mean-reversion. Trades are blocked when Hurst exceeds this threshold.
Heatmap As-Of Date
i
Show the heatmap as it looked on a specific date. Leave blank for latest available data.

Diagnostics
i
Rolling beta measures the log-price elasticity: how much T2 log-price moves per unit of T1 log-price. The CV (coefficient of variation) measures beta stability — lower is more stable. Hurst below 0.5 indicates mean-reversion in the spread.
i
Half-life estimates how many days it takes for the log-spread to revert halfway to its mean. Shorter is better for trading — values above ~60 days suggest slow or unreliable reversion. Spikes indicate instability.
